Mechanics



In order to figure out where to start, we had to do research. Firstly, we looked for existing products that functions similarly to how we would imagine our product to work and discovering how said product functions and applying those principles to our own design. One of these products included the flashlight crank. The flashlight crank utilizes a process called electromagnetic induction to produce electricity. It does this by rubbing the surface of a magnet against a wired coil. When this is done it pulls electrons from the magnetic field, generating energy.

We decided on using this process for our bike motor by attaching a chain from the rotating bike wheel to a motor. We planned on utilizing this process for our invention because of its simplicity and effectiveness.

Afterwards, we needed to make sure enough electricity being generated was enough. When trying to determine this we used various websites; one of which being the "OMNI Calculator" this site allowed us to calculate the amount of wattage generated from cycling. It used many averages such as the average US male and US female weight, average bicycle weight etc. After discovering our wattage output, about 45 watts.

We moved onto modeling what our prototype. We took inspiration from other devices that attempted to complete what we were trying to do as a base and improving upon it. Though currently our product generates enough energy, it is not consistent. We had to take into account inconsistent speeds and overworking the battery over time. We needed to find a way to keep the electric current stable without over stimulating the battery.

We found that by transferring our electricity generated by the motor to a regulator we were able to keep the electricity stable and discharge extra energy. This not only fixes our problem but ensures that the life of the battery lasts longer.
